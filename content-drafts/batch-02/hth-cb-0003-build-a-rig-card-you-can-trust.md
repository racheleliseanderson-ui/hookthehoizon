# Build a Rig Card You Can Trust

**Brief ID:** HTH-CB-0003  
**Format:** Field kit / setup record  
**Intelligence relationship:** HTH-I1 + HTH-I3  
**Status:** Complete editorial draft; manufacturer-specification, contributor, safety, conservation, privacy, visual, and printable-card review required  
**WordPress status:** Not created; hold outside WordPress

Most gear notes are too vague to teach you anything.

“Medium rod. Ten-pound line. Small jig. Worked well.”

Three months later, that record leaves out the details that explain success, failure, or uncertainty. Which rod model? What line material and diameter? What leader? Which knot? What lure weight? What drag setting? What conditions? What failed first?

A trustworthy rig card records the setup as a system.

## Isolated gear lists hide compatibility

Rod, reel, line, leader, terminal tackle, lure, knot, drag, and technique transfer load, movement, abrasion, casting force, and control through one connected system.

A setup can look reasonable when each item is considered separately and still contain a weak or unsupported connection.

A useful card answers:

- what exactly was used;
- which specifications are verified;
- which values are estimated or unknown;
- what the intended technique and condition were;
- where the weakest supported link is;
- what happened in the field, if a real field record exists;
- what should be changed, inspected, or tested next.

## Record identity before opinion

For each component, record:

- manufacturer;
- product name;
- model or series;
- relevant specification;
- source;
- checked date;
- whether the value is printed, published, measured, estimated, or unknown;
- condition.

Do not fill gaps with confidence. **Unknown** is more useful than an invented specification.

## Define the job of the rig

Record the intended technique or presentation, broad species category where relevant, general water type, expected cover or current, depth, wind, casting distance, lure or bait range, and whether the priority is sensitivity, control, abrasion resistance, casting, or durability.

Avoid “all-purpose.” All-purpose usually means the trade-offs have not been named.

## Build the component chain

### Rod

Record model, length, power, action, line range, lure range, condition, and role.

### Reel

Record model, size, capacity, gear ratio, documented drag information, condition, and compatibility with the rod and line.

### Main line

Record brand, product, material, labeled strength, diameter, age, installation date, and visible condition.

### Leader

Record material, strength, diameter, length, connection, and reason for use.

### Connections

Record every knot, splice, swivel, clip, ring, or connector; what it joins; how it was assembled; whether it was inspected; and any slippage, deformation, or damage.

### Terminal tackle and lure

Record hook size and style, weight, lure or bait, hardware, and total working weight where relevant.

## Identify the weakest supported link

The weakest supported link may be:

- an unverified specification;
- damaged or aged line;
- a knot joining dissimilar materials;
- unknown hardware;
- a lure near or outside the rod’s documented range;
- uncertain drag behavior;
- insufficient leader for the stated abrasion context;
- an uninspected component.

Classify each part as **verified**, **observed**, **inferred**, **unknown**, or **failed**.

Then write:

> The setup is limited primarily by __________ because __________.

This sentence is more useful than a universal compatibility score.

## Add a field-test record only when a real test exists

After actual use, record:

- date and general conditions;
- privacy-safe water type;
- weather and water observations;
- technique and duration;
- changes during the session;
- casting and presentation behavior;
- abrasion, slippage, tangles, drag behavior, hook damage, or lure loss;
- what held up;
- what remains uncertain;
- what must be inspected before reuse;
- the next test.

Do not record only catches. A no-catch session can still produce useful compatibility evidence.

Do not create a field-test record from specifications, retailer copy, a generated scenario, or unreviewed memory. First-person statements require the contributor’s real record and final wording review.

## Protect location privacy by design

The public rig card should not require precise coordinates. Use broad categories such as small river, large river, reservoir, natural lake, inshore saltwater, surf, public pier, or general coastal region.

Exact locations and sensitive clues should be excluded from public payloads, analytics, screenshots, images, file metadata, exports, and cross-publication handoffs.

## The rig card template

### Identity

- rig name;
- date;
- intended job;
- general environment;
- privacy classification.

### Components

- rod identity and documented ranges;
- reel identity and capacity;
- main line identity, age, and condition;
- leader identity and function;
- knots and connections;
- terminal tackle, weight, and lure or bait;
- total working weight where relevant.

### Compatibility assessment

- verified relationships;
- conditional relationships;
- inferred relationships;
- unknown components;
- weakest supported link;
- inspection stop;
- non-purchase or existing-gear alternative.

### Field test

- conditions;
- technique;
- duration;
- what held up;
- what failed;
- what changed;
- what remains unknown;
- next test.

## What not to assume

Do not treat labeled strength as a guarantee of actual system performance. Knots, wear, damage, installation, drag, shock, angle, and environmental conditions affect the real setup.

Do not exceed documented equipment guidance. Do not use a rig card as a substitute for inspection, current official regulations, weather and water warnings, boating requirements, or qualified instruction.

Do not promise catches. Compatibility is about whether the system is coherent and inspectable, not whether fish will cooperate.

## The next useful move

Choose the setup you use most often and complete the card without guessing. Every blank is a useful discovery.

The goal is not to make the rig look impressive. It is to know what the system is, where its limits are, and what the next field test should actually test.
