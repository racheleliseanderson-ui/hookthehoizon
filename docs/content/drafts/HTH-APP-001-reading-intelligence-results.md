## Reader problem

A fishing tool can look very sure of itself even when the information underneath it is incomplete, old, or only partly relevant. Readers need a fast way to tell the difference between a useful starting point and a result that should stop them from acting.

## Strongest format

Application-result explainer + field checklist + reusable result-key module.

## Draft title

# Before You Trust the Result: What Compatible, Conditional, Unknown, and Mismatch Mean

## Opening

A result card is not a fish oracle.

It is a structured answer to the information you gave the tool, the evidence the tool is allowed to use, and the rules that were current when the result ran. That can be genuinely useful. It can also be incomplete.

The important part is not whether the screen looks confident. The important part is whether you can see **what matched, what did not, what is missing, and what you should verify next**.

Here is how to read every Hook the Horizon Intelligence result without treating it like a guarantee.

## The four labels

### Compatible

**Meaning:** The declared equipment, conditions, or data fit the rule boundaries currently documented for the task.

**It does not mean:**

- the setup is perfect;
- a fish will bite;
- the water is safe or legally accessible;
- a device measurement is accurate;
- the recommendation fits every species, technique, or personal skill level.

**What to do next:** Check the limitations, confirm current manufacturer and regulatory information, then test one variable at a time.

**Example:** A rod, line, leader, and lure may fall inside documented operating ranges for a stated technique. That is a compatibility result, not a durability test or catch prediction.

### Conditional

**Meaning:** The result may work, but one or more boundaries need attention.

A conditional result should always name the condition. Examples include:

- the equipment relationship is near a documented limit;
- the presentation can reach the target depth only with a controlled speed or angle;
- a device capability exists, but the evidence is limited to a documented prototype or narrow operating condition;
- a source is aging and should be checked before relying on it.

**What to do next:** Read the stated condition before using the result. Do not mentally upgrade “conditional” to “basically compatible.”

### Unknown or insufficient information

**Meaning:** The tool does not have enough reliable information to decide.

This is not a failure. It is the honest result when a specification, observation, source date, device identity, water condition, or inventory record is missing.

**What to do next:** Supply the missing information only when it is safe and appropriate. Exact fishing locations are not required and should not be entered.

### Mismatch or stop

**Meaning:** A documented rule conflict remains, or the requested conclusion would exceed the available evidence.

Examples include:

- a lure or load falls outside a declared equipment range;
- a candidate presentation conflicts with the selected equipment system;
- a device claim is not supported by primary documentation;
- a prohibited or location-sensitive field appears in the input;
- the source is unavailable and no safe cached result exists.

**What to do next:** Stop, change the conflicting variable, or use the no-purchase alternative. A mismatch should never be hidden by a high popularity score, product rating, or attractive result card.

## Five things to check before acting

### 1. What information did the tool actually use?

Look for the declared method, water type, conditions, equipment, observations, inventory, or device identity. A result cannot account for information it never received.

### 2. How fresh are the sources?

A current source can become aging, stale, unavailable, or subject to migration. That matters most for regulations, access, water data, product specifications, firmware, and device exports.

USGS states that its legacy WaterServices endpoints are scheduled for decommissioning in early 2027 and applications must migrate to the newer Water Data APIs. That is exactly why a result needs a source date and service-status label instead of silently assuming an endpoint will remain unchanged.

### 3. Is the claim documented or merely plausible?

A manufacturer page can document that a feature is claimed. It does not automatically prove field accuracy. A patent, prototype, contributor recollection, or marketing phrase must not be presented as a validated commercial capability.

The Federal Trade Commission’s substantiation standard requires a reasonable basis for objective product claims before those claims are communicated. Hook the Horizon applies the same practical principle editorially: claim strength cannot exceed evidence strength.

### 4. What is still unknown?

Unknowns belong in the result, not buried in fine print. Typical unknowns include water temperature, exact model identity, firmware version, calibration status, direct insect observations, depth, structure, or whether the item is actually in the reader’s inventory.

### 5. What is the no-purchase move?

A useful result should help before it sends anyone shopping.

Examples:

- shorten the pause before changing the lure;
- reduce one load variable while keeping the rest of the system stable;
- use the closest owned profile and document the difference;
- enter an event manually when a device export is unsupported;
- collect one additional observation before choosing a hatch candidate.

## How the labels apply in each lab

### System Compatibility

The result explains whether parts of a fishing system fit documented ranges and relationships. It should identify the exact conflict or uncertainty. It does not prove field performance, safety under every load, or manufacturer warranty coverage.

### Presentation Planner

The result ranks controlled presentation experiments from the reader’s owned inventory. It should explain the matched conditions, equipment compatibility, evidence state, freshness, failure signals, and first variable to change. It does not predict fish activity or guarantee a catch.

### Hatch Match

The result ranks plausible insect or forage candidates using broad region and date bands, direct observations, source freshness, and available tying materials. It does not verify that a hatch is occurring. Biological records require qualified sourcing and review before public use.

### Rig Signal

The result separates documented device capabilities from prototype, inferred, ambiguous, or unsupported claims. It also explains which fields may be imported, which remain local, and which are prohibited. It does not prove bite detection, species identification, fight classification, or catch prediction.

## Field checklist

Before using a result, answer:

- [ ] Can I see the input assumptions?
- [ ] Can I see the evidence state?
- [ ] Can I see the source review date?
- [ ] Are stale, unavailable, or migrating sources visible?
- [ ] Are unknowns stated plainly?
- [ ] Is the equipment relationship compatible, conditional, unknown, or mismatched?
- [ ] Is there a no-purchase alternative?
- [ ] Is the next test limited to one major variable?
- [ ] Does the result avoid exact-location collection or exposure?
- [ ] Do I still need to verify current regulations, access, weather, water, or manufacturer information?

## Cautions

- Never use an application result as the sole basis for a safety, access, legal, or emergency decision.
- Do not enter exact coordinates, private-water names, access points, parking coordinates, or identifiable map links.
- Do not infer a commercial device capability from a patent, prototype, recollection, or undated marketing statement.
- Do not treat a stale or unavailable source as current because an old value still appears plausible.
- Do not convert a plausible hatch candidate into a biological fact without qualified evidence.
- Do not let ratings, popularity, reporting volume, or affiliate value override compatibility and evidence.

## Next action

Open the result details and find three lines before doing anything else:

1. **Why this result appeared**
2. **What remains unknown**
3. **What to verify or change first**

If those lines are missing, the result is not ready to guide a decision.

## Visual brief

Create an accessible, text-first result key rather than a decorative chart.

Required components:

1. Four stacked result cards: Compatible, Conditional, Unknown, Mismatch.
2. Each card includes: meaning, not-a-guarantee statement, next action, and one example.
3. A horizontal evidence strip: documented → reviewed → observed → inferred → unknown → do not publish.
4. A source-state strip: fresh → aging → stale → outage/unavailable.
5. A “one variable at a time” field-test diagram.
6. No maps, coordinates, identifiable waters, fabricated device screens, pseudo-text, or moodboard imagery.
7. Full keyboard order, high contrast, screen-reader labels, print version, and reduced-motion behavior.

## Disclosure and evidence requirements

- Display ruleset version and evaluation time.
- Display source IDs or human-readable source labels.
- Display source freshness and availability state.
- Distinguish official documentation, reviewed secondary material, contributor observation, user observation, inference, dispute, unknown, and do-not-publish states.
- State whether an item was owned, field-tested, manufacturer-documented, prototype-only, or not independently verified.
- Place affiliate disclosure before any commercial route.
- Do not claim testing unless a dated field record exists.
